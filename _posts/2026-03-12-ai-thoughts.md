---
title: "What 10-year-old me would think of AI"
---

If 10-year-old me could see AI now, I don't think his first reaction would be excitement.

I think it would be disbelief.

Not because he wouldn't love it. He would. But because the gap between the world he knew and the world we live in now is ridiculous.

When I think about that younger version of myself, I don't start with AI. I start with the Commodore 64.

## It started with waiting

At first, the Commodore 64 was just a game machine.

My dad and I used to get games from a guy called Rex. I didn't know it at the time, but they were pirated copies, cracked and passed around in that casual way software used to move. We'd bring them home, load them up, and wait.

And wait.

I still remember trying to get *Buggy Boy* going and not really knowing whether it was loading or whether absolutely nothing was happening. We'd press Enter or Return as if maybe that was the missing step, when really the machine was just taking its sweet time.

That was one of my earliest lessons about computers.

They felt powerful, but mysterious.

They didn't explain themselves. A lot of the experience was waiting, guessing, trying things, and hoping.

## Then I found BASIC

That changed everything.

My dad borrowed a library book on BASIC programming for the Commodore 64, and I worked through it line by line, typing every command out by hand. No copy and paste. No autocomplete. No safety net. Just a kid, a book, and a machine that either obeyed or didn't.

That was the moment computers stopped being something I used and started being something I could direct.

That's a massive shift.

The feeling was immediate. Creativity. Control. Wonder. Excitement. As soon as I realised I could make the machine do what I wanted, my brain started sprinting ahead to what else might be possible.

Most of what I built was exactly what you'd expect from a 10-year-old. I remember creating a rough little interface for a local gas station, complete with a logo and a pretend payment flow. It was crude. It was basic. But it was mine.

I also remember not really knowing how to save what I'd made, or not having the right setup to save it properly, so I'd rebuild the same ideas again and again from scratch.

Even then, the important part had already clicked.

Computers weren't just for consuming things.

They were for creating things.

## Reaching for something I didn't understand

Around the same time, I became vaguely aware of the internet, or at least some early idea of it.

I didn't understand that I needed a modem. I would still try to connect anyway and feel disappointed when nothing happened. Some part of me clearly believed it might just magically work.

Looking back, I love that memory.

Because it says something useful.

I didn't understand the system yet, but I was already reaching towards it.

That's been a recurring pattern in my life with technology. I don't need to fully understand the whole thing before curiosity kicks in. Curiosity usually comes first. Understanding catches up later.

## AI would have felt like magic

From where I sit now, the distance between that world and this one is almost absurd.

It's not just that computers are faster or more powerful. It's that so much of the friction has disappeared.

Back then, I had to copy code out of a library book by hand to make a machine do something simple. Now I can describe an idea in plain English and have AI help turn it into software in minutes.

That's not a minor upgrade.

That's a completely different relationship between thought and execution.

If 10-year-old me could see that, he'd feel like he'd skipped several centuries.

Not just because of AI either. The whole environment would be hard to process. The screens. The operating systems. The speed. The fact that I can speak to a machine and have it talk back intelligently. The fact that language itself now works like an interface.

Compared to the Commodore 64, it's outrageous.

## The bigger shock wouldn't be the technology

The bigger shock would be the life built around it.

At 10, I couldn't have imagined that within another 10 years I'd be building websites for a living. I definitely couldn't have predicted that I'd spend more than two decades making a living through the internet, staying close to technology as it kept changing shape.

That part still does my head in a bit when I stop and look at it directly.

Because from the outside it might look like a career path.

It didn't feel like that at the time.

It felt more like following the thing that kept pulling me forward.

That's what I think 10-year-old me would actually recognise. Not the tools. Not the terminology. Not the scale. The instinct.

## The real achievement

What would he be most proud of?

Not any single milestone.

Not money. Not titles. Not any one project.

I think he'd be proud that I stayed with it.

I kept learning. I kept adapting. I kept moving with the technology instead of getting left behind by it.

That's not nothing.

To stay engaged across more than 30 years of change, from a Commodore 64 to AI-assisted creation, is something I'm genuinely proud of.

The tools changed beyond recognition.

The instinct didn't.

That's the real thread running through all of this. The kid typing BASIC out of a borrowed library book and the adult using AI to build faster are not two different people.

Same person.

Different leverage.

## Why AI feels different

That leverage matters.

AI has changed how creation feels for me.

I feel more ambitious. Less blocked. More capable of moving from idea to reality before the energy disappears. The friction between imagination and execution has dropped massively, and that changes what I'm willing to attempt in the first place.

That matters more than people realise.

Sometimes the biggest value in a new tool isn't just speed. It's momentum. It's the fact that you stay in motion long enough to turn a loose idea into something real before doubt, complexity, or distraction kills it.

AI does that.

It lowers the barrier between "this would be interesting" and "this now exists."

For someone wired the way I am, that's a big deal.

## But there is a catch

I don't think the story is as simple as saying everything is better now.

Of course some things are better. Dramatically better.

But AI also raises a real question: what happens when people stop learning the fundamentals?

What happens to depth of understanding when the machine does more of the heavy lifting?

What happens to craft?

What happens to judgement?

Because that's the part people can get lazy about.

If AI writes the code, shapes the words, generates the image, or gives you the plan, you still need enough understanding to know whether it's good, whether it's true, whether it fits, and whether it's actually solving the right problem.

Otherwise you're not really creating.

You're just pressing buttons and hoping.

And funnily enough, that takes me right back to the Commodore 64.

## What I would tell him

If I could speak directly to 10-year-old me, I wouldn't overcomplicate it.

I'd say: keep going.

Keep messing around with computers. Keep following the curiosity. Keep trying things before you fully understand them. That instinct is going to take you a long way.

You don't need to change much.

That's probably the most satisfying part of this whole reflection.

For all the insane changes in technology, the core thing that mattered was already there early. The curiosity that made me wait for games to load, type out BASIC programs by hand, and try to connect to something I didn't yet understand is still the same curiosity driving me now.

The machines have changed almost beyond recognition.

The curiosity hasn't.
